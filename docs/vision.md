# Product vision

## The problem

OSINT/GEOINT analysts who geolocate armed-conflict events have no dedicated, open, professional tool to archive and share their work.

The current workflow is fragmented: geolocations are posted to Twitter, lost in the feed, with no structured format and no place to centralize them. The few dedicated tools that exist tend to be closed to part of the community and ship with dated interfaces — leaving room for an open, professional alternative.

## The solution

An open, simple, professional web platform where analysts can reference, archive, and visualize their geolocations of armed-conflict events worldwide.

## Primary persona — the OSINT/GEOINT analyst

**Who they are.**
An enthusiast (amateur or professional) who collects media (images, videos) from armed conflicts via open sources: Twitter/X, Telegram, Signal, satellite-imagery providers, etc. They use objective elements present in the media to determine the precise geographic location of an event.

**Where they live.**
Mostly on Twitter/X and Discord. The community is international, English-speaking, and very active around major conflicts (Ukraine, Middle East, sub-Saharan Africa…).

**Their current frustrations**
- No standardized format for sharing a geolocation
- Work scattered across Twitter, hard to retrieve and archive
- Existing tools are closed (restricted access, not open to the whole community)
- Interfaces feel rough and non-responsive — they don't reflect the quality of the work being done

**What they're looking for**
- A single place to centralize and archive their geolocations
- A structured, readable format to present their proof
- An interactive map to visualize all events
- A tool they can recommend to their community without embarrassment

## Guiding principles

1. **Openness** — the platform must be accessible to as many people as possible, with no artificial barriers. (The closed-beta invite-code system is a temporary mechanism to ramp the user count safely; it's retired in Phase 3 — see [roadmap.md](roadmap.md).)
2. **Simplicity** — the UI must be intuitive. Posting a geolocation should take less than two minutes.
3. **Quality** — the design and experience must match the seriousness of the community.
4. **Neutrality** — the platform references geolocated facts, not political stances.
